Why Adjusted Financials?

Financial statements often contain "noise"—one-off items that distort the true operational performance. Adjusted (Normalized) Financials remove these anomalies (like impairment charges, restructuring costs, or windfall gains) to reveal the sustainable Core Earnings capacity of the business.

Defining "One-off" Items

In adjusted financial statements, "one‑off" means an income or expense item that is unusual, non‑recurring, and not part of the company's normal, ongoing operations.

Typical one‑off items include:

  • Gain or loss on sale of a business or major asset
  • Restructuring costs (severance, plant closure, reorganization)
  • Large legal settlement or fine that is not expected to recur
  • Impairment/write‑down of assets
  • Disaster‑related losses (e.g., flood, fire)
  • Exceptional tax gains or losses

CS & Governance Role

While management prefers Adjusted metrics for investor presentations, the Company Secretary must ensure:

  • Transparency: Any "Adjusted" figure must be clearly reconciled with Reported (GAAP) figures (as per Ind AS 1 / SEBI norms).
  • Consistency: The definition of "Core" shouldn't change year-to-year just to look better.
  • No Misleading: Adjustments shouldn't mask recurring operating costs as "one-offs".

Interpretation Tip

If Adjusted PAT is consistently higher than Reported PAT for 3+ years, investigate if "exceptional items" are actually routine operating costs being hidden.
